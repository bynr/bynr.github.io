Context

I was first introduced to the concept of risk by a co-worker when faced with the task to help manage a project for the first time of my career.

He wanted to know all the tasks that had to be done to complete a project, and which of those were particularly risky.

By risk, he meant anything unexpected that could impact the project, and especially the deadline: tasks that cannot be estimated, out of our control, too uncertain…

He proposed to create a document to summarize this information, keep it updated daily and share it with the project management team. The ideal format would be one where one look would suffice to know which items were most at risk and should be tackled right away.

In this post, I will dive into the nuances of the problem, and some solutions considered.

Problem definition

We wanted to differentiate risks with an impact on the deadline, and on different aspects of the project.

Types of risks:

  • Schedule-Risk → Cannot be estimated and can move the schedule.
  • System-risk → Pay close attention to this!

Users: project manager Frequency of update: daily

There might be many items on the list, but without going through all items, the most importants ones should pop out immediately.

Once we identify most dangerous tasks, we can start tackling them: schedule those, assign, discuss.

Purpose:

  • know overall status
  • identify critical tasks to be prioritized

The risk list is not a task board! All tasks that can be decomposed into a kanban board are by definition already discussed, estimated and scheduled and are not risky anymore.

A risky item might end up being broken down into tasks and delegated to the team, after being properly de-risked.

Purpose is to identify all risks before they explode. A typical situation you want to avoid is something you that explodes and you realize needed to be tackled from the start, but only realize when you hand over the release to the customer. This is something you want to avoid at all costs.
